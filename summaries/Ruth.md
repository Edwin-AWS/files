# Ruth

## Introduction

🆕 Book* Ruth
  
TITLE
The book of Ruth forms an appendix to the book of Judges, and an introduction to the two historical books of Samuel. It receives its title from the name of the person whose story it tells. Ruth was a Moabitish woman, and naturally her name is not Hebrew. The derivation and meaning of the name are uncertain, though some relate it to mean, “to associate with,” and thus mean “friend,” or “friendship.”

✍🏽 Authorship 
The Authorship of the book of Ruth is debated but is attributed to Samuel. A careful study of the book of Ruth has led many scholars to the conclusion that the book is likely to have been of preexilic origin. This is all that can be said as to the date of the writing of the book of Ruth. The written form of the book of Ruth, as we now have it, probably originated in the time of David himself, and it seems to fit best in the early days of his reign. According to Jewish tradition, as recorded in the Talmud, the prophet Samuel wrote not only the books bearing his name but also the book of Judges and that of Ruth. This would explain the position of the book of Ruth at the close of the book of Judges and preceding Samuel (see on Judges 17:1; 18:29).  

💡 Historical Setting 
The setting of the story is explicitly stated in the opening words of the book: “In the days when the judges ruled, … there was a famine in the land.” Yet this statement is by no means definite, for there was more than one famine in the land of Palestine during the time of the judges. However, by comparing the genealogy of David as given in the last verses of the book of Ruth with David’s genealogy as given in the first chapter of Matthew, we discover Boaz’ mother listed as Rahab. It is supposed this to be the Rahab of Jericho (see Matthew 1:5). 

✍🏽 Themes 

#️⃣ The chief purpose of the book of Ruth is to give information concerning the immediate ancestors of David, the greatest of the kings of Israel, the one in whose line was to come the Messiah. 
#️⃣ Ruth also presents a most appealing picture of the blessings of the ideal home. There are two institutions that have come down to us from before the fall of man—the Sabbath and the home.  The Sabbath is not Jewish, for, as the Creator Himself said, in Mark 2:27. However both the Sabbath and the home have become the special objects of Satan’s attacks.
#️⃣ The relationship of mother-in-law and daughter-in-law is a subject of amusement to many. But not that of Ruth and her mother-in-law, Naomi. After a sojourn of ten years in the land of Moab, Naomi, decided to return to Judah. Ruth, with a devotion that speaks almost as much for Naomi as it does for Ruth herself, broke all ties of home and kindred to accompany her. 
#️⃣ Finally the story is replete with superb examples of faith, piety, humility, industry, and loving-kindness revealed in the ordinary occurrences of life. Thus we have in the story of Ruth, a significant comment on a part of the genealogy of Christ (see Matthew 1:4–6).

💯 Note
 Israel should have been prepared by a study of this narrative to understand God’s plan for the salvation of individuals of all nations who would do as Ruth the Moabitess did—accept the God whose character had been represented to them by His servants. It was God’s plan that many would thus be so transformed in character as to be prepared individually to become citizens of the eternal kingdom of Christ (see E. G. White: Christ Object Lessons 290).

## Ruth 1


### Summary

Elimelech, his wife Naomi and their two sons Mahlon (sickness) and Chilion (consumption) left Bethlehem-Judah (house of plenty) for Moab in search of food, since there was famine in Bethlehem. Elimelech and his sons died, leaving Naomi with the Moabite daughters-in-law, namely Opah and Ruth. Naomi decided to return home after God visited Bethlehem-Judah, encouraging her daughters-in-law to remain. Opah agreed, but Ruth persisted in going with Naomi. Upon arrival, Naomi feels her life is empty and changes her name to 'Mara' (meaning bitter) to reflect her grief and pain.


### Key Lessons


#️⃣ Judgment follows Disobedience (v 1 -6). A famine in the land of Bethlehem, Judah was one of the judgments  which God  had threatened to bring upon Israel. (Judges 17: 6;18: 1; 19: 1; 21: 25).  This was the period where "Everyman did that, which was right in his own eyes, ....."

#️⃣ God's grace  is for all, there was plenty in Moab yet famine in Israel. Salvation belongs to both Jews and Gentiles (Israel and Moab).

#️⃣ We should not be unequally yoked to non-believers (2 Corithians 614).

#️⃣ Faithfulness can be practiced even in a foreign land as was done by Naomi, who cleaved to her God till returning to Judah.

#️⃣ Divisions and conflicts can arise between in-laws, Ruth and Naomi were always in harmony (Matthew 10:35). We ought to live in harmony with our in-laws.

#️⃣ God is a restorer; His redemption is possible for anyone regardless of their past. Ruth's declaration was a turning point from idolatry to the worship of the true God (v 16-18).

#️⃣ God is never against us, but interpreting life through bitterness can distract our relationship to God and people.


> 🙏 **Prayer:** Heavenly Father, help us to hide in you, so that we acquire spiritual fullness, deeper love, and a knowledge of Jesus Christ. Amen.

## Ruth 2


### Summary

Boaz, a relative of Elimelech, shows kindness, protection, and blessing upon hearing Ruth's loyalty  to Naomi. He insists Ruth gleans barley from his field alone and also from sheaves already gathered. Ruth shares all that she gleans with her mother-in-law.


### Key Lessons


#️⃣ Ruth displays humility by gleaning in the fields. As Christians, we must be humble in all circumstances.

#️⃣ Ruth loved not to eat bread whilst idle, we should be industrious as Children of God.

#️⃣ God wisely orders what seem to us small events and those that appear altogether uncertain, still are directed to serve his own glory and the good of his people. (Ellen G. White: Thoughts From the Mount of Blessings p 235.2)

#️⃣ Boaz's kindness to Ruth symbolizes Jesus Christ's kindness to sinners (1 Samuel 24: 19; Psalms 57: 1; 63: 7).

#️⃣ Ruth is a hardworking model for young people. She paid respect to her mother-in-law. Children should obey their parents so that their days are numbered (Exodus 20:12, 1 Timothy 5:4  ,Matthew 15:6).

#️⃣ Ruth gave an account of the day to her mother-in-law, in like manner children should be accountable in all their daily actions. As Christians, we ought to have answers to all our actions.

#️⃣ Ruth, a Moabite (enemies of Israel), childless, and widowed chose to save the true God of Naomi. No matter how our past could be, let's have hope and trust  in God, for His promises are not wavering (Titus 2: 14.)


> 🙏 **Prayer:** Thank you our heavenly Father for lessons from Ruth where you remind us of unconditional love. Help us to love unconditionally. Amen.

## Ruth 3


### Summary

Naomi advises Ruth to seek Boaz as a potential kinsman-redeemer. She instructs Ruth to bathe, anoint herself, dress neatly, and go to the threshing floor where Boaz sleeps after work. Ruth, in a humble manner; follows Naomi’s guidance by lying at Boaz’s feet. She identifies herself and requests that he become her kinsman-redeemer. Boaz responds honourably, explaining that there is a close relative who must be consulted first. He assures Ruth that the issue will be settled.


### Key Lessons


**Genuine Love and Care**


1️⃣ Genuine Love and Care

#️⃣ Naomi and Ruth show true, selfless love - an example for Christians to follow.

#️⃣ Ruth listens to Naomi’s counsel, reminding children and believers to value godly guidance.

**Faith, Boldness, and Action**


2️⃣ Faith, Boldness, and Action

#️⃣ Ruth’s courage and obedience illustrate how believers must act in faith, not fear.

#️⃣ True relationships and marriages require honesty and transparency (Hebrews 13:4).

**The Kinsman-Redeemer**


3️⃣ The Kinsman-Redeemer

#️⃣ Boaz’s duty as a kinsman-redeemer reflects the law of redeeming a relative from slavery (Leviticus 25:47–49), foreshadowing Christ redeeming sinners from the bondage of sin.

#️⃣ Ruth covering herself with Boaz’s garment symbolizes how God covers us with His righteousness (Ezekiel 16:8).

**Spiritual Preparation and Grace**


4️⃣ Spiritual Preparation and Grace

#️⃣ Ruth’s act of bathing and anointing herself symbolizes the way believers present themselves as living sacrifices, transformed in mind and spirit (Romans 12:1–2).

#️⃣ Ruth’s poverty and widowhood did not disqualify her. Our weaknesses do not  disqualify us for God’s grace.

#️⃣ Believers may face hardship, but must look to Christ, their Redeemer, for security (Hebrews 12:2; Isaiah 45:22; Proverbs 3:5–6).

**Christ the True Redeemer**


5️⃣ Christ the True Redeemer

#️⃣ The closer relative who could not redeem Ruth represents the law, which cannot save.

#️⃣ Boaz represents Christ, who alone is able to redeem humanity.

Naomi symbolizes Israel, displaced yet awaiting restoration.

#️⃣ Just as Boaz’s relationship with Ruth granted him the right to redeem, Christ’s blood grants Him the right to redeem us.


> 🙏 **Prayer:** Dear Lord, grant us wisdom to seek Your will and courage to take steps of faith when You call us to act. In moments of waiting, like Ruth and Naomi, teach us patience and trust. In the mighty name of Jesus, our ultimate Kinsman-Redeemer, we pray. Amen.

## Ruth 4


### Summary

Ruth 4 completes the story. Boaz gathers ten elders to witness a legal transaction concerning Naomi’s land and Ruth’s future. The close relative declines the duty of kinsman-redeemer because it might endanger his inheritance. Boaz therefore redeems the land and marries Ruth. God blesses their union with a son, Obed, who becomes the grandfather of King David, establishing the lineage through which Jesus Christ would come. Naomi’s sorrow is turned to joy, as she receives this blessing through Ruth and Boaz.


### Key Lessons


**The Kinsman-Redeemer (Go’el)**


1️⃣ The Kinsman-Redeemer (Go’el)

#️⃣ Boaz fulfilled the duties of a kinsman-redeemer, according to the law (Deuteronomy 25:5).

#️⃣ He redeemed the land of an impoverished Israelite, fulfilling the command of Leviticus 25:25.

**Christ Our Ultimate Kinsman-Redeemer**


2️⃣ Christ Our Ultimate Kinsman-Redeemer

#️⃣ Jesus fulfills every aspect of redemption:

⏩ Redeemer who breaks bondage (Hebrews 2:15.)

⏩ Giver of eternal life (John 1:4, 1:12–13.)

⏩ Redeemer of our inheritance (Ephesians 1:11–14.)

⏩ The righteous Judge (John 5:27.)

⏩ The close relative could not redeem - the law cannot save; only Christ can.

⏩ Boaz’s transparency with the elders teaches us that relationships and families must value openness to avoid conflict.

⏩ Faith is a public and communal journey—helping one family blesses an entire community.

**Ruth**


3️⃣ Ruth

#️⃣ Showed humility and gratitude, not self-pity. Believers should not dwell on their problems, but bring everything to God in prayer (Philippians 4:6).

⏩ Became free to marry; likewise, Jesus has set us free from the law of sin and death.

⏩ Once barren, Ruth became fruitful - those in Christ bear the fruit of the Spirit (Galatians 5:22).

⏩ Ruth’s story ends in blessing - believers who persevere in Christ will share in the glorious ending of the New Heaven and New Earth (Revelation 21:4).

**Naomi**


4️⃣ Naomi

#️⃣ Naomi (meaning “pleasant,”) returned to the promised land and experienced God’s restoration.

⏩ From bitterness (“Mara”), God brought hope and a secure future.

God is faithful and brings joy to those who trust Him.

⏩ We must pray even in dark seasons (Joel 2:25; Job 42:10; Isaiah 61:7).

#️⃣ Acts of kindness have unseen impact - Naomi loved Ruth, Boaz loved Ruth, and God blessed generations (Ephesians 4:32; Proverbs 19:17).


> 🙏 **Prayer:** Thank You, Lord, for reminding us that all things work together for good to those who love You. Help us trust and wait on You always. In Jesus’ name, Amen.
